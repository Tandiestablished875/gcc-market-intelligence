# Customer Development Interview Guide — GCC

> Last verified: May 2026. Sources: practitioner adaptations of The Mom Test (Fitzpatrick), Lean Customer Development (Alvarez), regional sales-leader interviews, Madinah Tech Cultivator cohort experience.

## Purpose

This is the operational companion to `references/culture/customer-development.md`. That file explains WHY GCC customer development is different. This file gives you the scripts, templates, and operating instructions to actually run interviews.

## Before any interview

### Pre-interview checklist

1. **Who am I talking to?** Specific name, title, employer, country. Background-check via LinkedIn — what was their previous role, where did they study, how long have they been in their current role.
2. **What is their role in the buying process?** Decision-maker / influencer / champion / user / gatekeeper. If unclear, plan to ask in the meeting.
3. **What is my hypothesis?** Write one specific testable hypothesis before the meeting. ("I believe that hospitals in Saudi Arabia will pay $X/year for real-time patient-flow analytics because of Vision 2030 healthcare quality KPIs.")
4. **What disconfirming evidence am I open to?** If they say X, my hypothesis is wrong. Be specific.
5. **What's the next step I want?** Pilot? Intro? Reference? Information? Be explicit with yourself.
6. **Language: English or Arabic?** Default to English unless context indicates otherwise. Have an Arabic-speaking advisor on standby for senior or government meetings.
7. **Time:** 45–60 minutes is typical. Don't book a 30-minute slot for a first meeting — too short for relationship-building.
8. **Location:** their office > their preferred venue (hotel coffee shop, restaurant) > video call. In-person carries 3–5x the signal of video for first meetings.

## The interview structure (60 minutes)

### Minutes 0–10: Connection
**Goal:** establish you respect them as a person, not just as a buyer.

Questions:
- "How did you end up in this role?" / "What was your path?"
- "How are you finding [their company / the sector] these days?"
- "Are you originally from [country] / based here long?"

Don't:
- Look at your watch.
- Pull out a slide deck.
- Push toward the agenda.

### Minutes 10–30: Discovery
**Goal:** understand their actual problem space, not pitch your product.

Open with: "I'm here to learn, not to pitch. Would it be okay if I asked some questions about how your team handles [problem area]?"

Then:

#### Q1 — Day in the life
"Walk me through how a typical week looks for your team in [problem area]. What's working well, what's frustrating?"

Listen for: specific moments of friction, current workarounds, ad-hoc tools.

Follow-up: "When that frustration hits — what's the actual workaround?"

#### Q2 — Past behavior, not hypotheticals
"Looking back at the last 6 months, was there a moment where your team really felt the pain of [problem area]? What happened?"

GCC-specific follow-up: "How did your team explain that to leadership — did anyone push for change?" Reveals internal politics + budget unlock conditions.

#### Q3 — Existing solutions and competitors
"How do you handle this today? What tools or vendors are involved?"

Follow-up: "Have you looked at other vendors? What was your impression?"

If they name competitors: "What did you like about them? What didn't quite fit?"

This is gold — direct competitive intelligence from the buyer's mouth.

#### Q4 — Decision-making structure
"In your organization, when someone wants to bring in a [your category] vendor, what does that typically look like? Who initiates? Who signs off?"

Gentler than "who has budget authority?" — gets the same answer.

Follow-up: "How long does that typically take from first conversation to signed contract?"

#### Q5 — Vision / strategic alignment
"How does this connect to your team's KPIs / your company's strategic priorities for this year?"

In KSA: probe for Vision 2030 program alignment. In UAE: We the UAE 2031, D33, sector-specific (National AI Strategy, Net Zero 2050). In Qatar: NDS3. In Bahrain: Vision 2030 / Tamkeen alignment. In Kuwait: Vision 2035 / sector mandates. In Oman: Vision 2040 / Future Fund Oman alignment.

If they can name a specific Vision-pillar KPI → strong buying intent.
If they wave at "digital transformation" generically → weak buying intent.

### Minutes 30–45: Light positioning
**Goal:** test whether your product seems relevant — without pitching.

Approach: ask them about your product, don't tell them about it.

#### Q6 — Solution-shape probe
"If you imagine the ideal solution to [problem area] — not constrained by what's available today — what would it look like?"

Listen for: features they'd want, workflow shape, integration points.

#### Q7 — Differentiation probe
"What would make you choose vendor A over vendor B in this category?"

Listen for: their actual decision criteria. Often surprising.

#### Q8 — Localization probe
"Is data residency / Arabic language support / ICV / Saudization-friendly hiring relevant for your team?"

In KSA / UAE / Oman this almost always matters. In Bahrain / Kuwait / Qatar varies.

### Minutes 45–55: Mutual fit
**Goal:** if there's mutual interest, structure a clear next step.

Approach:
"Based on what you've shared, here's what we do briefly: [60-second pitch]. Does this resemble what you're looking for, or do we sound off?"

Then:
- If they engage: "What would have to be true for us to do a 6-week pilot together?" (Note: 6 weeks not 6 months — GCC buyers respond to specificity and speed.)
- If they're cool: "What's a sector / use-case where you'd see this being more relevant?"
- If they ask about price: "Our typical pilot is $X for 6 weeks; production deployments are usually $Y/year. Where would your team be most comfortable?"

### Minutes 55–60: Wrap and next step
**Goal:** specific, named next step on calendar before you leave.

Don't accept:
- "Send us a proposal." (Usually polite "no.")
- "Let's stay in touch." (Polite "no.")
- "Inshallah we'll be in touch." (Often polite "no.")

Do push for:
- "Could we schedule a 45-minute follow-up with [specific person they mentioned] in 2 weeks?"
- "Would you be open to a half-day technical deep-dive with your engineering team in [specific month]?"
- "Could you introduce me to [specific person they mentioned]?"

Capture:
- Their preferred follow-up channel (email / WhatsApp / LinkedIn message).
- Confirmed date for next interaction.
- Names of additional people to loop in.

## Specific phrase templates

### Opening phrase
> "Thank you for making time. I'm here to learn — I won't pitch unless you ask me to. Would it be okay if I asked you a few questions about how your team approaches [problem area]?"

### Permission-to-probe phrase
> "I appreciate that's confidential — let me ask differently. How do teams like yours in [sector] typically handle that?"

### Re-asking after deflection
> "I noticed you mentioned [generic answer] earlier. Could you give me a specific example of when this came up for your team?"

### Surfacing real concerns
> "If you were briefing your CEO / Sheikh / Minister on this category, what would you tell them about the gaps in the current state?"

### Closing without commitment
> "Based on this conversation, I think the most useful next step might be [X]. Does that match what you're thinking, or would something else be more useful?"

## Post-interview checklist

Within 24 hours of the meeting:

1. **Write up the interview notes.** Use a consistent template:
   - Name, role, company, date, location.
   - Their stated problem.
   - Their workarounds.
   - Competitors mentioned.
   - Decision structure.
   - Strategic alignment / Vision pillar.
   - Stated next step + date.
   - Your hypothesis: confirmed / disconfirmed / partial.
   - Surprises (always have something here — if you have no surprises, you weren't listening).
2. **Send a thank-you note within 24 hours.** Not 48. Not 72. See `assets/meeting-followup.md` for templates.
3. **Connect on LinkedIn** (with a personalized note referencing the conversation).
4. **Update your CRM / pipeline tool** with explicit next-step date and confirmation status.
5. **Decide whether to invest more time.** Use the 3-question test:
   - Did they share a specific Vision-pillar KPI?
   - Did they name a specific colleague to loop in?
   - Did they describe a concrete past moment of pain?
   
   If yes to 2/3 → invest more. If yes to 0–1/3 → polite follow-up but don't burn time.

## Interview templates by buyer type

### Government / ministry buyer (B2G)

Adjustments:
- Frame around Vision 2030 / We the UAE 2031 / NDS3 / Vision 2040 — these are their actual KPIs.
- Probe Etimad / Tamm / Tender Board registration: "If we were to engage formally, what's the typical onboarding path through your procurement team?"
- Ask about ICV, Saudization, Emiratisation, Omanisation requirements specific to vendor selection.
- Be aware: government buyers often can't sign within fiscal year if RFP wasn't planned. Probe timing: "What's your procurement cycle look like for next year?"

### Sovereign-fund portfolio company

Adjustments:
- Confirm whose portfolio they're in (PIF, Mubadala, ADQ, ICD, QIA, OIA). This affects governance.
- Probe: "Is there a fund-led vendor preference list we should know about?"
- Many SWF-portfolio companies have hub-region preferences (PIF → Riyadh, Mubadala → Abu Dhabi). Plan accordingly.

### Family conglomerate (B2B)

Adjustments:
- The first conversation is often with a junior family member or an external advisor (often a Western expat CFO or COO). Treat them as a champion, not a final decision-maker.
- Don't expect specific budget numbers in first meeting; that comes in meeting 3 or 4.
- Probe: "What's the relationship pattern with [family principal] — do you bring them in early or later in the process?"

### Bank / financial-services buyer

Adjustments:
- Probe regulatory standing: "Are you working with SAMA / CBUAE / FSRA / DFSA / QFCRA / CBB / CBK / CBO directly on this, or is it internal innovation budget?"
- Banks have annual procurement cycles tied to fiscal year (mostly Gregorian). Time your push.
- ICV-equivalent rules apply: GCC banks increasingly require local-citizen workforce minimums.

### System integrator / channel partner (not a customer but a multiplier)

Adjustments:
- Different conversation entirely. Frame around: "How do we sell THROUGH you, not to you?"
- Probe: "What categories do you currently bring in vs build internally?" reveals gaps.
- Most GCC SIs want 20–40% margin for partner-channel sales. Build into your pricing model.

## Common interview failures

### Failure mode 1: pitching instead of listening
You started pitching in minute 8. By minute 20 they're polite but disengaged. By minute 45 they say "send us a proposal" to end the meeting.

**Fix:** Set a hard rule with yourself: no pitching until they ask. Force them to ask.

### Failure mode 2: missing the actual decision-maker
You spent 60 minutes with an IT manager who has no budget authority and no internal political weight.

**Fix:** Ask Q4 (decision-making structure) within first 20 minutes. If the answer reveals you're talking to a non-decision-maker, redirect the meeting to ask about how to reach the actual decision-maker.

### Failure mode 3: ignoring the politeness signal
They said "very interesting," "send a proposal," "Inshallah we'll be in touch." You thought it was a yes. Three months later they don't return your emails.

**Fix:** Use the decoding table from `references/culture/customer-development.md`. Don't count anything as a yes until you have a calendar item.

### Failure mode 4: rushing the relationship
You followed up the same day with a 4-page proposal and a request for a contract within 30 days. They went silent. The deal died.

**Fix:** Match their pace. Following up too fast signals desperation. Wait 24+ hours. First follow-up should be thank-you only, not a proposal.

### Failure mode 5: speaking down to junior contacts
You insisted on speaking only to the "senior" person. The junior contact, who was actually the product owner, got annoyed and didn't loop you back in.

**Fix:** Treat junior contacts with respect. They are often the actual buyers; the senior person is the budget rubber-stamp.

## What outside founders should know

- **One good interview > five mediocre ones.** Slow down. Spend more time with fewer people if those people are the right people.
- **Don't conduct interviews during Ramadan business hours unless the buyer invites you.** Iftar interviews are gold; daytime intrusions during Ramadan are tone-deaf.
- **Multi-party interviews are common.** Don't be thrown if 5 people walk into your "1:1." Acknowledge each, learn each role, address the room.
- **Some of your best signals come AFTER the meeting.** A follow-up email response within 6 hours = strong buying intent. Silence for 2 weeks then a generic reply = polite no.

## Verify before committing capital

- **Interview signal:** validate strong signals by talking to 2+ people in the same organization. Single-interview signals are weak.
- **Pilot commitment:** verify the named decision-maker actually has budget authority before sinking development effort.
- **Pricing intelligence:** validate with 3+ buyer-side sources before adjusting your price.
